# Reflexive Journal Template

**Purpose:** Critical self-reflection on research decisions, assumptions, biases, and positionality.

**When to use:**
- After major decisions (see decision_log.md)
- At end of each phase
- When encountering conflicting evidence
- Before finalizing outputs

## Example Entry

```markdown
# Reflexive Journal Entry #1

**Date:** 2026-03-03
**Track/Phase:** gdpe_0002_evidence_anchoring / Phase 1
**Trigger:** NZ evidence base extremely weak (all "Very Low" quality)

---

## Context

Populating NZ evidence register revealed almost no quantitative evidence on genetic discrimination and insurance. All evidence is qualitative (HRC Inquiry submissions) or extrapolated from Australia/international studies.

---

## Positionality Check

### My background relevant to this decision
- **Professional:** Health economist with clinical background
- **Geographic:** New Zealand-based, Australian collaborations
- **Theoretical:** Bayesian decision analysis, welfare economics
- **Personal:** No known family history of genetic conditions

### Potential biases
- [x] Availability bias (overweighting AU evidence due to familiarity)
- [x] Expertise bias (assuming AU findings apply to NZ)
- [ ] Confirmation bias
- [ ] Status quo bias
- [ ] Cultural bias
- [ ] Other: Proximity bias (AU data is easier to access)

---

## Critical Reflection

### What assumptions am I making?

1. That AU and NZ insurance markets are sufficiently similar for extrapolation
2. That HRC Inquiry submissions are representative of broader NZ experience
3. That wider priors adequately capture the additional uncertainty

### Where could I be wrong?

1. NZ market dynamics may differ significantly (higher concentration, different regulation)
2. HRC submissions may over-represent affected individuals (selection bias)
3. Wide priors may still be too narrow given fundamental uncertainty

### What perspectives am I missing?

1. Māori perspectives on genetic testing and insurance
2. Pacific community views
3. Insurer perspective (not represented in evidence)
4. People who DID undergo testing despite concerns (silent majority)

### How might my positionality affect this?

My AU connections make me more familiar with AU evidence, potentially overweighting its relevance. My health economics training predisposes me to quantitative approaches, but the evidence is fundamentally qualitative.

---

## Power and Privilege Considerations

### Who has voice in this research?
- [x] Researchers (me)
- [ ] Policymakers
- [x] Industry (insurers) - via FSC documents
- [ ] Affected communities (people with genetic conditions) - limited
- [ ] Clinicians
- [ ] Other: [specify]

### Whose voices are absent or marginalized?

1. Māori and Pacific voices (no specific evidence)
2. People who experienced discrimination but didn't report
3. People who declined testing for other reasons (cost, access, personal choice)

### How am I addressing power imbalances?

1. Explicitly acknowledging evidence gaps
2. Using wide priors to reflect uncertainty
3. Planning VOI to identify high-priority evidence gaps
4. [Need to do more] - consider community engagement

---

## Emotional Response

### How do I feel about this decision/evidence?

Uncomfortable. Frustrated. Imposter syndrome.

### What is driving these emotions?

Building policy models on very weak evidence feels irresponsible. Worry that recommendations could be wrong and affect real people.

### Are emotions influencing my judgment?

Yes - may be over-correcting with very wide priors. But also, the discomfort is appropriate epistemic humility.

---

## Alternative Viewpoints

### What would a critic say?

"This is policy-based evidence-making. You're building a model to justify a predetermined policy position on weak foundations."

### What would someone with lived experience say?

"Your model reduces my experience to a probability distribution. The uncertainty you're quantifying is my life."

### What would a policymaker need to know?

"That the evidence is this weak should be the story. The model shows we're making policy in the dark."

---

## Changes Made

### Based on this reflection, I will:

- [x] Use even wider priors for NZ parameters
- [x] Make evidence gaps prominent in all outputs
- [x] Add reflexive notes to decision log
- [ ] Consult with Māori health researchers (TODO: Phase 2)
- [ ] Consider community engagement for VOI prioritization

### What I'm choosing NOT to change (and why)

Proceeding with Phase 2 despite weak evidence. Rationale: Making uncertainty visible is better than ignoring the problem. VOI will show where evidence matters most.

---

## Commitments

### For future work, I commit to:

1. Engage with Māori health researchers before Phase 3
2. Make evidence limitations prominent in all policy briefs
3. Consider qualitative methods to supplement quantitative modelling
4. Reflect on positionality at each phase review

### Review date

End of Phase 2 (after calibration)

---

## Notes for Decision Log

- Decision: "NZ evidence adapted from AU with wider priors"
- Decision: "Very low quality evidence accepted with wide priors"
- Decision: "Māori data sovereignty principles acknowledged"

---

**Signed:** [Initials]
**Date:** 2026-03-03
```

## Usage Instructions

1. **Create entries** at `context/reflexive_journal/entry_#[number].md`
2. **Link to decision log** - transfer key points to `context/decision_log.md`
3. **Review periodically** - especially before major outputs
4. **Be honest** - this is for you first, others second
5. **Act on reflections** - don't just document, change practice
